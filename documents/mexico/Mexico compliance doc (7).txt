7. Standard Transaction Process
The standard Mexican property purchase follows a defined sequence involving the notario público as the central legal authority. Unlike in the U.S., the notario in Mexico is a government-appointed official — not a private attorney — who has binding responsibility for the legality and tax compliance of the transaction.

7.1 The Notario Público — Central Role
The notario público (Notary Public) in Mexico is a government-appointed professional with legal training who serves simultaneously as a legal authority and a government agent. The notario's role is far broader than in common-law countries: they verify title, confirm all taxes are paid, validate the seller's authority, assess AML compliance, and officially register the deed. All real estate transactions in Mexico must be executed before a notario.
* The buyer has the right under NOM-247 to select the notario who will formalize the purchase.
* Notario fees: typically 0.75–1.5% of property value. Fees are regulated by state aranceles (fee schedules).
* Notario cannot proceed without proof of ISAI payment, clear title, and satisfied LFPIORPI requirements.
LFPIORPI NOTARIO THRESHOLD — July 2025 Reform (Previously Missing): The July 2025 reform reduced the notario's LFPIORPI reporting threshold for real property transfers from 16,000 UMAs to 8,000 UMAs (approximately MXN $904,000 at 2025 UMA values). Any property transfer at or above this value now triggers mandatory notario due diligence and SAT reporting. Platforms displaying property values should flag listings at or above this threshold in any compliance workflow or transaction management tool.
