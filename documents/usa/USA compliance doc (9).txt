9. FinCEN Residential Real Estate Rule — Effective March 1, 2026
The Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), a bureau of the U.S. Treasury Department, implemented a nationwide Residential Real Estate (RRE) Rule on March 1, 2026. This rule replaces the prior system of Geographic Targeting Orders (GTOs), which applied only in select cities with purchase price thresholds. The new rule has no geographic limitations and no minimum purchase price — it applies nationwide to any qualifying transaction.
SCOPE: The RRE Rule applies when all three conditions are met: (1) residential real estate is transferred, (2) the buyer is a legal entity or trust — NOT an individual taking title in their own name, and (3) the transaction is non-financed, meaning no mortgage from a regulated bank secured by the property.


9.1 What Is a 'Non-Financed' Transfer
A transfer is non-financed unless it involves credit that is both secured by the property being transferred AND extended by a financial institution subject to federal AML program and SAR requirements. The following all qualify as non-financed and are therefore reportable if the buyer is an entity or trust: all-cash purchases, seller-financed transactions, privately financed transactions, and loans from non-bank lenders not subject to AML obligations.


9.2 Covered Property Types
* Single-family houses, townhouses, condominiums, and cooperatives (including units in large multi-unit buildings)
* One-to-four family residential properties
* Vacant land where the purchaser intends to construct a one-to-four family residential structure
* Mixed-use and apartment buildings
* Applies in all 50 states, Washington D.C., Puerto Rico, and Native American lands


9.3 Who Must File — Reporting Person Cascade
The filing obligation falls on designated 'reporting persons' involved in the closing or settlement — not on buyers or sellers directly. The cascade works as follows: the first person on a defined list who is involved in the transaction must file. The list includes: the closing or settlement agent that prepares the closing statement, the title insurance company, the escrow company, the buyer's attorney, and the seller's attorney, in that priority order.
ATTORNEY STATES: Attorneys in states where attorneys routinely handle escrow and title — including New York, Connecticut, Delaware, Georgia, Massachusetts, North Carolina, South Carolina, Vermont, and West Virginia — should expect to be designated as the reporting person in many transactions.


9.4 What Must Be Reported
* Property address and legal description
* Closing date and purchase price
* Payment method
* For entity buyers: full name, address, jurisdiction of formation, EIN, and beneficial owner information for all individuals with 25%+ ownership or substantial control
* For trust buyers: trustee information, grantor information, beneficiary information, and information on any individuals with authority to control decisions on behalf of the trust


9.5 Filing Deadline and Retention
* Report must be filed by the LATER of: (1) the last day of the month following the month of closing, OR (2) 30 calendar days after closing date.
* The reporting person must retain a copy of the filed report for five years.
* FinCEN does not permit submission of reports with missing required information — collect all data before closing.


9.6 Exemptions
* Transfers occurring by reason of death or inheritance
* Transfers incident to divorce or dissolution of marriage
* Court-ordered transfers
* Bankruptcy proceedings
* Certain Section 1031 exchange transactions (narrowly defined — verify on a per-transaction basis)
NOT EXEMPT: Individual buyers taking title in their own name are not covered (no reporting required regardless of payment method). But the moment an LLC, partnership, corporation, or trust is the buyer, the rule applies to non-financed transactions.
